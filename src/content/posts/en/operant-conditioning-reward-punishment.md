# Operant Conditioning: Reward and Punishment Explained

## Quick answer

Operant conditioning is learning in which behaviour changes because of its consequences, such as reinforcement or punishment.

If you remember only one line for an exam, remember this: **Operant Conditioning is about Skinner, reinforcement, punishment.**

## Simple explanation

Think of psychology like a friendly detective for human behaviour. It does not only ask “what happened?” It asks “why did it happen, how can we observe it, and what evidence supports the answer?”

A student gets praise after completing homework, so the student is more likely to complete homework again.

For beginners, the safest method is to connect every term with three things:

1. the definition,
2. one daily-life example, and
3. one exam keyword.

## Real-life example

Imagine an exam student preparing for a competitive test. The student reads a concept, tries practice questions, gets feedback, and changes the next study plan. This small situation can show attention, memory, motivation, emotion, and learning at the same time. ## Common mistakes and confusions

- **Memorising without examples:** definitions become weak when you cannot attach a real case.
- **Mixing similar terms:** many psychology terms sound close, but exams expect a clean difference.
- **Using moral judgement instead of psychology:** psychology explains behaviour with evidence; it does not simply call people good or bad.
- **Ignoring context:** the same behaviour can have different meanings in different situations.
